Work Zones.

Construction is New York's single largest source of attributed delay — 188.6M vehicle-hours since 2018, about twenty-two times the delay attributed to crashes. Unlike a crash, a work zone is known in advance: this page tracks where that delay lands, how long zones run, and how much of it is planned work that can be scheduled around.

Most of it is planned — which means most of it is schedulable.

This is the planned-work half of the network — the construction, maintenance, and emergency-operations zones the incidents page hands off. We attribute delay to each zone the same way: join its affected segments to NPMRDS probe speeds, then price it at a vehicle-class-weighted value of time. Because work zones are known ahead of time, nearly all of this delay is addressable through lane-closure timing and corridor coordination.

// methodology
How work-zone delay is measured

Work-zone events are TRANSCOM's Construction, Maintenance, and Emergency Operations categories (by nysdot_sub_category), filtered to New York — the planned-work counterpart to the reactive incidents on the incidents dashboard. Delay is attributed per zone by joining its affected TMCs to NPMRDS probe speeds and subtracting the recurrent baseline; the share-of-total compares against the statewide excessive-delay attribution (2019–2020 not computed). Dollars use a vehicle-class-weighted value of time — passenger $52, single-unit truck $42, combination truck $77 per vehicle-hour — weighted by each segment's AADT truck mix (≈$53 blended, ~$58 on the freight corridors where most work-zone delay sits). Exposure (lane-mile-hours) is estimated from TMC length × active duration, not a lane-closure permit feed. This dashboard covers work-zone mobility; work-zone safety — crashes, secondary crashes, worker injuries — is a separate dashboard in development.
