Theory · No. 01

On placing.

An essay on representation primacy — M. Tessera, 2026

A tessera is a single tile in a mosaic — the smallest unit of a deliberately composed surface. Each tessera is small and ordinary; the composition is what's beautiful and durable.

The instinct to build a software product around tools is older than the web. Many of the great content-management systems were first conceived as tools for managing content: an admin, a wysiwyg, a publish button. The shape of the data was an implementation detail of the shape of the tool.

We have come, slowly, to think the order is exactly inverted. The shape of the data is the shape of the site. A page is a row. A chart is a row. A map is a row. A join is two rows pointing at each other. The product of Tessera is a small, durable representation that survives the renderings placed against it.

This is, of course, an old argument. Tufte said it; the relational database said it; the typeface foundry said it about glyph metadata. We are not the first to say it. We are saying it again because the present generation of site-building tools has, in our judgement, drifted from the original idea.

“The shape of the data is the shape of the site. A page is a row. A chart is a row. A map is a row.

The mosaic, the floor, the room

The Cosmati pavements of medieval Italy are, in this sense, a productive metaphor. The floor is mostly stone — quiet and pale and ordinary. The inlaid pattern is the punctuation. The room is not the pattern; the room is the surface that the pattern punctuates.

A Tessera site should feel like a Cosmati floor. Mostly stone. A small amount of inlaid colour. A great deal of considered negative space. Nothing whooshes.

The argument continues, at length, in print — but the first claim is the only one that matters here. The shape of your data is the shape of your site.
