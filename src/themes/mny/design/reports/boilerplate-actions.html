The verdict

Legitimate to reuse.
Too generic to act on.

Boilerplate is not a duplicate. A duplicate is the same action in the same place, recorded twice — a mistake to delete. Boilerplate is the same action text reused across different places: a standard mitigation measure a planning consultant applies to every municipality in an annex. It is entirely legitimate. The trouble is that it is low-information — the identical wording in 45 towns tells you nothing about what any one of them will actually do.

827 templates account for 3,467 rows — 18.9% of the dataset. A single template, “Public Awareness Program,” appears 449 times. And the templates themselves drift: that one name carries 30 slightly different descriptions, so you can’t even count “how many public-awareness actions exist” cleanly. Reuse without a controlled catalog quietly becomes un-countable.

Reliance is bimodal by county. Ontario’s plan is 82.8% template; Saratoga’s 63.9%; Greene’s 66.8%. Meanwhile Sullivan, Onondaga, New York City and Putnam are 0% — every action locally authored. Like duplication, boilerplate is a fingerprint of who prepared the annex, not of the hazard.

Tellingly, the template-heavy counties are not the duplicate-heavy ones. The two data-quality problems are almost disjoint: some annexes were imported twice, others were built from a template library. Neither is the actions themselves being wrong — both are artefacts of how the plans were assembled.

Shaping playbook

Make the reuse work for the dataset

The goal is not to delete boilerplate — it is to turn identical text into locally meaningful records. Four moves, in leverage order:

1 · Consolidate the pure programs

“Public Awareness Program,” “Substantial Damage Procedures,” “Update NYS Building Code” are genuinely uniform. Author each once as a shared program and reference it per jurisdiction, instead of copy-pasting it 40+ times. One edit then propagates everywhere.

2 · Localize the place-specific ones

“Protect Power Lines,” “Improve Stormwater Management,” “Critical Facility Flood Outreach” describe real local projects wearing a generic name. Give each a site, a local cost, and a town-specific problem — the fields that are 0% today.

3 · Standardize the drift

One name with 30 descriptions can’t be counted. Fold template variants into a controlled catalog of named measures so “how many awareness actions statewide” becomes an answerable question.

4 · Flag template vs. local

Mark each action as adopted-template or locally-authored so dashboards can separate “we adopted the standard program” from “we identified a specific project.” Start with the top ~25 templates, which cover a large share of all 3,467 rows.
